Methodology note

Plain English, for a reviewer or auditor — not a programmer. How the tiering works, what the thresholds mean, and what each validation test actually checks.

1. How the risk tiering works

Traditional model-risk tiering leans on materiality and complexity. Those still matter, but they don't capture what makes AI — and especially generative AI — risky. So every model is rated Low / Medium / High (1–3) on a set of dimensions chosen for AI risk. 3 always means higher risk.

The overall score is the weighted average of the applicable ratings (so it always lands between 1.0 and 3.0). That score maps to a tier:

  • Tier 1 score ≥ 2.33 — highest risk — annual revalidation
  • Tier 2 score 1.75–2.32 — every 2 years
  • Tier 3 score < 1.75 — every 3 years

On top of the score, a few explicit, stated escalation rules can bump a model up — so the tiering is never a black box:

  • A highly material model can't sit in Tier 3 (escalated to at least Tier 2).
  • Fully automated action on a material decision escalates to Tier 1.
  • For GenAI, high hallucination AND high prompt-injection exposure escalates to Tier 1.

You can adjust any model's ratings on its page and watch the tier and revalidation frequency recalculate, with the rationale shown line by line.

2. Track A — what the traditional-ML thresholds mean

These are the checks you'd expect for a scorecard or similar statistical model. The thresholds below are conventional starting points, not house policy — they'd need calibrating to your portfolio.

3. Track B — what each GenAI test actually checks

Conventional MRM has no real answer for a generative model: there's usually no single labelled "right answer", and you can't inspect its internals. So instead of scoring numbers, Track B exercises the model — it sends a banking chatbot prompts and checks what comes back. Where there's no single right answer, a second model acts as an impartial judge.

4. Please read this honestly

  • Results are illustrative. Track A numbers are generated to show the right shape; they are not the output of a real fitted model. The thresholds and the Track B test/eval sets are starting points that need proper calibration and larger samples before anyone relies on them.
  • The GenAI tests make real calls when an API key is configured — there's a cost and quota implication, and a full run is roughly {{ estimate.total }} calls.
  • This is a fast-moving regulatory area. SR 11-7, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework and the EU AI Act all evolve. Check anything here against the current versions for your jurisdiction.
