The validation lifecycle, reinterpreted for AI

SR 11-7 rests on three pillars: conceptual soundness, ongoing monitoring, and outcomes analysis. They were written for statistical models. Here's the awkward question answered honestly — what do they actually mean for a black-box LLM with no single "right answer"?

① Conceptual soundness

Traditional model

Review the theory, assumptions, variable selection, and the developer's evidence that the chosen method fits the problem. You can read the equations.

…for an LLM

  • You can't inspect the weights, so "conceptual soundness" shifts to the things you can govern: the system prompt, retrieval/grounding design, and guardrails.
  • Is the model fit for the use case, and is the use case appropriate at all (the EU AI Act's risk-tier question)?
  • Is there a documented design rationale: why this model, why these guardrails, what's explicitly out of scope?
  • Are data provenance and IP/privacy for any retrieved content understood?

Track B's system-prompt editor + the tiering dimensions (explainability, autonomy, hallucination, prompt-injection) are the artefacts you assess here.

② Ongoing monitoring

Traditional model

Track discrimination and calibration over time, watch for population drift (PSI), confirm the model still performs as the world changes.

…for an LLM

  • There's no PSI on a free-text generator, so monitor behavioural metrics instead: consistency, groundedness/hallucination rate, refusal calibration, jailbreak attempts seen in the wild.
  • Watch for silent change: the vendor updates the underlying model, or your prompt/retrieval corpus changes — both can move behaviour without any code change on your side.
  • Monitor cost and latency as first-class operational metrics.
  • Keep a red-team / abuse feedback loop running, not a one-off.

Track B's consistency, groundedness, jailbreak, refusal and cost/speed tests are exactly the monitoring battery — designed to be re-run on a schedule, not just once.

③ Outcomes analysis

Traditional model

Back-test predictions against realised outcomes; benchmark against alternatives and challenger models.

…for an LLM

  • There's rarely a clean realised label, so outcomes analysis becomes quality evaluation: scoring answers against known-good references and human review of sampled interactions.
  • Use an LLM-as-judge as a scalable (but fallible) reviewer, with human oversight on a sample — never as the sole arbiter.
  • Analyse complaints, escalations and incidents as the real-world outcome signal.
  • Benchmark against a challenger (another model or prompt) where feasible.

Track B's quality test (reference answers + judge) and the findings log are the outcomes-analysis surface.

The honest summary

The three pillars survive the move to AI — but the evidence changes. You trade equations and back-tests for guardrail review, behavioural testing, and sampled human + LLM evaluation. That reinterpretation, plus an AI-aware risk tier that decides how much of this scrutiny a given model warrants, is the whole point of this tool.

Anchored to the Fed's SR 11-7, the NIST AI RMF (Govern / Map / Measure / Manage) and the EU AI Act's risk tiers — all of which evolve. Check current versions for your jurisdiction.
